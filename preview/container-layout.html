Primitives · structural

Container & Layout

Two atoms that wrap every other primitive. Surface owns visuals (background, border, padding, radius). Layout primitives (Stack / Row / Grid / Split / Center) own geometry (gap, direction, alignment). They never overlap in theory — but Surface has an escape hatch (data-layout) for the most common pattern: a bordered box that also arranges its children.

Surface · escape hatch

The 80% case is "a bordered box that stacks its kids with a gap". Rather than force two elements, Surface accepts data-layout="stack|row|grid|split" + data-gap. The surface becomes a layout parent in addition to its visuals. No separate wrapper needed.

two elements (strict)
<div class="surface" data-elev="raised" data-pad="md">
  <div class="stack" data-gap="sm">
    <div>A</div><div>B</div><div>C</div>
  </div>
</div>
one element (fused)
<div class="surface" data-elev="raised"
     data-pad="md" data-layout="stack" data-gap="sm">
  <div>A</div><div>B</div><div>C</div>
</div>

Rules

✓ Do
  • Fuse via data-layout when a surface's only job is to hold a gap'd list.
  • Use a separate layout primitive when the inner structure has its own logic (tabs, split headers).
  • Pair pad and radius from the same tier.
  • Let nesting auto-compact — don't set inner pad unless you want a specific exception.
✕ Don't
  • Stack three elevations visually — two layers max.
  • Use raw flex/grid inline styles. Always sample the scale via the primitive.
  • Mix two layout modes on one element (no data-layout="stack" AND a child .row wrapping everything).
  • Apply data-radius="xl" to pad="xs" containers.
